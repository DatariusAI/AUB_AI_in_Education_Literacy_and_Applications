Three Classroom Distinctions Worth Memorizing

Narrow AI vs. Generative AI vs. AGI. Narrow AI does one thing well (Netflix recommendations, Siri). Generative AI creates new content from patterns (ChatGPT, DALL-E, Copilot). AGI — human-level general intelligence — does not yet exist and is contested.

Prediction, not understanding. Generative AI produces outputs by predicting the next likely token. Fluent and confident answers are not the same as correct answers. Confidence does not equal correctness.

Tool, not teacher. AI accelerates planning, drafting, feedback, and analytics. It does not replace the educator's judgment about content accuracy, assessment fairness, or what an individual student needs.

Traditional AI vs. Generative AI — The Core Distinction

Traditional AI is analytical. It classifies, predicts, or decides. It is trained on labelled, structured data, scoped to one task per model, and produces categories or scores. Examples: email spam filters, fraud detection, loan credit scoring.

Generative AI is creative. It creates new content from learned patterns. It is trained on massive unlabelled data (web, books, code), is general-purpose, and produces text, images, audio, video, or structured data. Examples: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, DALL·E, Midjourney, Sora.

Key insight: Generative AI doesn't just analyze data — it creates new content from statistical patterns learned at enormous scale.

Five Limitations Every Educator Must Internalize

1. Hallucinations. AI generates plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information — fake citations, wrong statistics, invented events — with full confidence and no warning. Treat all AI output as a draft. Verify claims against authoritative sources.

2. Inherited Bias. Models learn from human-generated data and absorb societal biases (race, gender, culture, geography, class). Critically examine whose perspective is centered. Prompt for diverse viewpoints explicitly.

3. Over-Reliance. Students who use AI as a shortcut bypass the cognitive effort that produces genuine learning. The struggle IS the learning. Design tasks that require evidence of process, not just product.

4. Academic Integrity. AI-generated work submitted as a student's own is academic dishonesty, and detection tools remain unreliable. Rethink assessments toward personalization, process documentation, and oral or live components.

5. Data Privacy. Public AI tools may log conversations, train on inputs, or violate FERPA / GDPR. Never enter real student names, IDs, grades, or sensitive data into public AI tools. Use institution-approved platforms.

When to Use AI · When NOT to Use AI

Use AI for: drafting lesson plans and materials, brainstorming, generating question banks, analyzing structured data (e.g., student performance), explaining concepts at multiple levels.

Do NOT use AI for: situations where accuracy is critical and unverifiable, when human judgment and nuance are essential, when handling sensitive or emotional student situations, when student learning requires effort and critical thinking, when handling private or confidential data.

Key principle: Use AI as a support tool, not a replacement for professional judgment.

AI Architectures — The Five Generative Model Families

Lecture 2, Slide 9 introduces the five architectures behind today's generative AI tools. Each has a different mathematical core, a different training regime, and different educational use cases. Knowing which architecture sits behind a tool tells you what it's good and bad at.

Arch 1

Transformers

The backbone of most modern text AI. Uses self-attention to understand the relationship between words in a sequence. Powers all major Large Language Models (LLMs).

  • Powers: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama
  • Best for: Text generation, reasoning, code
  • Strength: Long-range coherence in text
Arch 2

Diffusion Models

Today's standard for image generation. Start from random noise and iteratively denoise it step-by-step into a clear image guided by a text prompt.

  • Powers: DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion
  • Best for: Visual aids, infographics, illustrations
  • Strength: High-quality image synthesis
Arch 3

GANs

Generative Adversarial Networks use two competing networks — a generator (the artist) and a discriminator (the critic) — to produce realistic content through adversarial training.

  • Powers: Deepfake generation, style transfer
  • Best for: Realistic synthetic media
  • Caution: Powerful misuse potential
Arch 4

VAEs

Variational Autoencoders compress data into a simplified latent representation and reconstruct it to create variations — like learning to squeeze a high-quality photo into a tiny "summary" and then build new things from that summary.

  • Powers: Image reconstruction, data interpolation
  • Best for: Variation generation, dimensionality reduction
  • Strength: Smooth latent-space interpolation
Arch 5

Multimodal Models

Handle and generate multiple types of data — text, images, audio — in one unified system. The current frontier of consumer-facing AI.

  • Powers: GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5, Claude 3
  • Best for: Image+text reasoning, real-world tasks
  • Strength: Cross-modal grounding

What each stage actually does

1. Data Collection (~1T+ tokens). Billions of web pages, books, academic papers, code repositories, and conversations are gathered, deduplicated, and cleaned.

2. Pre-Training (self-supervised). The model learns to predict masked or next tokens by adjusting billions of internal weights across thousands of GPU-hours. This is where most of the cost and learning happens.

3. Fine-Tuning (supervised). The model is trained on higher-quality curated data to follow instructions, answer questions, and stay on topic.

4. RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). Human raters score model outputs for helpfulness, accuracy, and safety. The model is refined using reinforcement learning to prefer outputs humans rated highly.

5. Inference (your prompt). You provide context and a question. The model generates a response one token at a time using learned patterns. This is the only stage you interact with directly.

#4 Tokens & Context Windows

From Slide 16: why prompt length, chunking, and session memory matter.

What is a Token?

AI models don't process words — they process tokens. A token is a unit of text (a word, part of a word, or a character), preferably 2–4 characters. Example: "misunderstanding" → mis · under · stand · ing = 4 tokens. The word "and" is 1 token.

Why this matters for educators

Prompt length matters. Longer, more detailed prompts almost always produce better outputs — context is not wasted, it is used.

Chunk long content. Paste one chapter at a time rather than an entire textbook. AI works better with clear, focused sections.

Memory between sessions. Most tools do not remember previous conversations by default. Each session starts fresh unless you re-paste the relevant context.

Prompt Engineering — The Five-Element Framework

From Slide 31: every strong educational prompt shares five elements — Role, Audience, Task, Format, Constraint. The framework is simple, but the difference in output quality between a weak and a strong prompt is dramatic.

Element 1

Role

  • Who should the AI act as?
  • e.g., "experienced middle school science teacher"
  • Anchors tone, vocabulary, depth
Element 2

Audience

  • Who is the output for?
  • e.g., "Grade 7 students with no prior background"
  • Drives complexity and examples
Element 3

Task

  • What exactly should be produced?
  • e.g., "design a 40-minute lesson on photosynthesis"
  • Be specific and bounded
Element 4

Format

  • What structure should the output take?
  • e.g., "objectives, activities, assessment"
  • Critical for downstream re-use
Element 5

Constraint

  • What rules or limits apply?
  • e.g., "use simple language, one real-life example"
  • Prevents drift and verbosity

#2 Weak vs. Strong — Side-by-Side

Two contrasting examples from Slide 31 showing how the framework transforms output quality.

Lesson Plan Prompt

WEAK: Create a lesson plan on photosynthesis

STRONG:

Act as an experienced middle school science teacher (ROLE), and design a 40-minute lesson on photosynthesis for Grade 7 students (AUDIENCE & TASK). Present the lesson in a clear structure including objectives, activities, and a short assessment (FORMAT). Use simple language and include one real-life example; assume students have no prior background knowledge (CONSTRAINTS).

Assessment Prompt

WEAK: Create quiz questions on climate change

STRONG:

Act as an assessment specialist in secondary education (ROLE), and create 5 multiple-choice questions on climate change for Grade 9 students (AUDIENCE & TASK). Provide one correct answer and three plausible distractors for each question (FORMAT). Ensure questions test understanding, not memorization, and avoid overly simple or ambiguous wording (CONSTRAINTS).

#3 Smart Tip — Use AI to Improve Your Prompts

From Slide 32: a recursive trick that often works better than writing the perfect prompt yourself.

Treat AI as a collaborator, not just a tool. Ask it to refine and structure your request before answering it.

Original: Create a lesson plan on climate change

Recursive prompt:

Act as a prompt engineer and improve the following prompt: "Create a lesson plan on climate change."

Then feed the improved prompt back to the same or a different AI tool. The output is almost always sharper.

Which one was AI-generated? — The Slide 33–34 Twist

Lecture 2 shows two paragraph-length explanations of climate change side by side and asks the class to identify which is human and which is AI. The answer is: both are AI.

The difference between them is not authorship — it is prompt quality. The shorter, weaker response came from "Explain climate change." The longer, more sophisticated response came from "Act as an experienced educator and explain climate change to high school students in a clear and slightly academic tone..."

The pedagogical takeaway: distinguishing AI from human writing is unreliable. The reliable thing is to own the prompt and own the editing.

Scenario

You are a teacher who has just completed a full semester with your class. You would like to understand how your students performed overall and identify any patterns in their results.

You have been asked to prepare a short report for the Head of the Educational Unit (or Academic Coordinator) to explain how your students are doing after you graded 4 quizzes of equal weights.

Using the sample dataset for a class of 8 students, analyze student performance and generate a short report (no more than 1–2 pages) that summarizes the overall situation of the class. After generating the AI response, briefly reflect on the following:

#5 Teacher Report

Final brief addressed to the Head of the Educational Unit — the deliverable submitted with this assignment.

To: Head of the Educational Unit

Subject: Class Performance Brief — Quizzes 1–4

Class average across the four quizzes is 75.1. The cohort is healthy at the top (Students A, C, E performing consistently above 82) but I want to flag two students who require different kinds of attention.

Student D has averaged 53.5 with a downward slope (55, 53, 60, 46). This is a sustained academic gap, not a single bad quiz, and I'd like to schedule remediation sessions and a parent meeting.

Student G showed a sharp drop from 91 (Q2) to 52 (Q3), with no recovery in Q4 (55). The pattern is too abrupt to be a content gap — I suspect a personal or behavioural change between weeks 2 and 3 and would like to meet with them privately first.

On the positive side, Students B and F showed strong upward trajectories on the later quizzes (B: 60→89, F: 70→93). Whatever changed for them — new study group, intervention, motivation — is worth understanding so we can replicate it.

Recommendation: Adopt a brief mid-term check-in (10-min one-on-ones) for any student showing a 15+ point swing between consecutive quizzes. The averages alone hid both Student G's collapse and Student F's improvement.

Prepared with AI-assisted analysis (Claude). Final interpretation, recommendations, and student-specific judgments are my own.

The following prompt was provided to Claude to assist in the analysis:

You are helping a high school teacher analyze a class of 8 students across 4 equally-weighted quizzes. Here is the data: Student | Q1 | Q2 | Q3 | Q4 A | 85 | 88 | 90 | 87 B | 60 | 65 | 70 | 89 C | 92 | 94 | 95 | 93 D | 55 | 53 | 60 | 46 E | 78 | 80 | 85 | 88 F | 70 | 72 | 91 | 93 G | 88 | 91 | 52 | 55 H | 72 | 73 | 75 | 77 Please: 1. Compute the class average per quiz and per student. 2. Identify students whose trajectory across the 4 quizzes is improving, declining, or stable. 3. Flag any unusual patterns (sharp drops, sharp rises) and suggest plausible explanations. 4. Suggest one concrete recommendation a teacher could act on. Keep the response under 300 words. #6 Reflection

Short post-mortem addressing the three required reflection questions.

What did the AI do well?

The AI handled the arithmetic and grouping reliably — per-quiz averages, per-student averages, and trajectory categorization were correct on the first pass. It also surfaced the polarization in the cohort and the late-quiz divergence pattern faster than I would have spotted manually. The structured prompt made the output easy to copy-paste into the brief with light editing.

What did the AI miss, and what did I modify?

Two things. First, the AI's initial recommendations were generic ("offer extra tutoring", "differentiate instruction") and didn't separate academic gaps from behavioural or situational ones. Student D and Student G have very different problems and need different responses — I had to add that distinction myself.

Second, the AI treated all averages equally and didn't weight recency. For a teacher writing a brief mid-semester, the last two quizzes matter more than the first two as a signal of where a student is heading. I rewrote the recommendation to focus on quiz-to-quiz swings rather than averages.

Would I rely on this report as a teacher?

Not as-is, no. I would rely on the AI for the descriptive layer — the numbers, the categorizations, the visual — but the interpretive and recommendation layer needs a teacher's judgment. The AI cannot know that Student G missed two weeks of class for a family reason, or that Student F joined a peer study group in Week 3. Those are the things that actually explain the data, and they live with the teacher, not the model.

The right framing is: AI as a fast first draft, teacher as the final author. This mirrors the lesson from Lecture 1 — confidence does not equal correctness, and educators remain responsible for accuracy and judgment.
